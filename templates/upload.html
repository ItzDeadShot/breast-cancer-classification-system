What's Next?

Now that you have received your breast cancer identification results, there are a few things you may want to consider:

If you would like to learn more about breast cancer, its causes, symptoms, treatment options, and other related topics, there are many resources available online and through your healthcare provider. Some reputable sources of information include:

  • American Cancer Society
  • Breastcancer.org
  • National Breast Cancer Foundation

If you have received a breast cancer identification result, it is important to discuss your results with your healthcare provider. They can help you understand what your results mean, answer any questions you may have, and recommend next steps based on your individual situation. But here is a brief summary of what the results mean.

  • If your result shows that your scan is potentially cancerous (malignant), we recommend that you consult with a medical professional as soon as possible to discuss further diagnostic testing and treatment options.
  • If your result shows that your scan is not cancerous (benign), we still recommend that you continue to receive regular breast cancer screenings as recommended by your healthcare provider. Breast cancer can develop at any time, so it is important to stay proactive about your health.

If you have not already done so, consider scheduling regular breast cancer screenings with your healthcare provider. The American Cancer Society recommends that women with an average risk of breast cancer start regular mammography screening at age 50 to 74, should get a mammogram every two years. Women who are 40 to 49 years old should talk to their doctor or other health care provider about when to start and how often to get a mammogram.
